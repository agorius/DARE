# DC-in-a-Can: Energy-Native Compute Infrastructure for Dispatchable Workloads  
### Unified Synthesis of the DARE Architecture

---

## Abstract

This paper synthesizes a set of design documents exploring a unified architecture for energy-native compute infrastructure. The system reframes computation not as a static service deployed on fixed datacenter resources, but as a **dispatchable load integrated directly into energy generation, storage, and thermal systems**.

The core thesis is that modern compute workloads—particularly AI inference, batch processing, and proof-of-work hashing—exhibit properties that make them suitable for **energy-state-aware scheduling**. By coupling compute orchestration with real-time energy availability, thermal constraints, and generator efficiency curves, it becomes possible to construct a system that maximizes economic output per unit energy while stabilizing physical infrastructure.

We present a unified model spanning power generation (gensets and stranded energy), DC distribution, thermal systems, workload classification, storage buffering, and multiprotocol scheduling. We identify gaps in formal modeling, control theory, and implementation detail, and propose next steps toward a deployable energy-compute operating system.

---

## 1. Introduction

Traditional datacenter architectures assume:

- Stable grid power
- Independent cooling systems
- Workloads scheduled for compute efficiency
- Energy as a fixed input constraint

However, emerging constraints in:

- stranded and curtailed energy markets  
- high-density AI inference workloads  
- distributed edge compute systems  
- generator-based microgrids  
- battery-buffered power systems  

invalidate this separation.

We propose a different framing:

> Compute is not a service running on energy systems. Compute is a controllable transformation process within an energy system.

This shifts the optimization target from computational efficiency to **thermodynamic and economic efficiency of energy conversion into compute output**.

---

## 2. System Overview

The architecture consists of five tightly coupled layers:

### 2.1 Physical Energy Layer
- Diesel/natural gas generators (e.g., 100kW V8 gensets)
- Grid or stranded energy sources
- Battery banks for buffering
- DC power distribution infrastructure

### 2.2 Thermal Layer
- Air or liquid cooling systems
- Heat rejection systems
- Thermal inertia modeling
- Fan power and parasitic losses

### 2.3 Compute Layer
- GPUs for inference workloads
- CPUs for orchestration and control
- ASICs for hashing workloads
- Mixed heterogeneous compute nodes

### 2.4 Workload Layer
- AI inference (latency-tiered)
- Proof-of-work hashing (steady-state load)
- Batch processing workloads
- Synthetic generation and background jobs

### 2.5 Control & Economic Layer
- Multiprotocol scheduler
- Energy-state-aware dispatch engine
- Economic optimization function
- Telemetry and feedback loops

## 3. Core Architectural Thesis

### 3.1 Energy-State-Aware Computation

Compute scheduling decisions depend on:

- Generator efficiency curves
- Battery charge/discharge state
- Thermal headroom
- Energy price signals
- Workload flexibility

Define system state:

\[
S(t) = \{E_g, E_b, T, C, W, P\}
\]

Where:
- \(E_g\): generator output state  
- \(E_b\): battery energy state  
- \(T\): thermal state  
- \(C\): compute capacity state  
- \(W\): workload queue  
- \(P\): external price signals  

Scheduling becomes:

\[
\max \; \mathbb{E}[V(W, S)] - \lambda \cdot C_{energy}(S)
\]

subject to thermal, electrical, and latency constraints.

---

### 3.2 Compute as Energy Transformation

Each workload is modeled as:

\[
W_i: E \rightarrow V
\]

Where:
- \(E\) = energy consumed
- \(V\) = economic value produced

Workloads differ in:
- interruptibility
- energy stability
- thermal signature
- value per kWh

This enables classification into **energy-compatible workload classes**.

---

### 3.3 Dispatchable Load Model

Compute behaves as a controllable demand source:

- Increases load during energy surplus
- Reduces load during energy scarcity
- Shifts execution across time and nodes

This makes compute a **grid-interactive resource**.

---

## 4. Key Subsystems

### 4.1 Multiprotocol Scheduler

A unified scheduler that:
- normalizes heterogeneous workloads into scheduling units
- assigns energy-aware priorities
- resolves conflicts across workload types
- enforces thermal and generator constraints

It operates as a **multi-objective control system**, not a queue manager.

---

### 4.2 Inference Pools

AI inference is structured as a pooled resource:

- capacity is dynamically allocated
- workloads are batched or delayed
- inference becomes elastic demand

This enables inference to function as:
- a flexible energy sink
- a high-value dispatchable workload class

---

### 4.3 Battery Bank as Control System

Batteries are not backup systems but:

- transient suppressors
- ramp-rate stabilizers
- thermal smoothing buffers
- energy arbitrage tools

They decouple:
- instantaneous generator output
from
- compute demand

---

### 4.4 Generator Integration Layer

Generators are treated as:

- continuous-load optimized systems
- not peak-following systems

Compute scheduling is used to:
- stabilize RPM
- optimize fuel efficiency
- reduce transient inefficiencies

---

### 4.5 Thermal Management Layer

Cooling is treated as a first-order constraint:

- fan power becomes significant at scale
- thermal inertia impacts scheduling
- workload placement affects thermal gradients

Thermal stability is co-optimized with compute dispatch.

---

## 5. Cross-System Dynamics

### 5.1 Coupled Control Loops

The system is a multi-domain feedback system:

- energy → compute allocation  
- compute → thermal load  
- thermal load → scheduling constraints  
- scheduling → generator load shaping  
- generator output → energy availability  

This forms a closed-loop cyber-physical system.

---

### 5.2 Time-Scale Separation

Different processes operate on different timescales:

| Timescale | System |
|----------|--------|
| ms | power stabilization |
| sec | thermal control |
| min | workload scheduling |
| hr | energy optimization |
| day | economic dispatch |

## 6. Gaps in Current Reasoning

### 6.1 Lack of Formal Control Theory Model
No explicit:
- state-space representation
- stability analysis
- control feedback design

### 6.2 Incomplete Thermal Modeling
Missing:
- airflow equations
- fan power curves
- heat transfer modeling
- liquid cooling dynamics

### 6.3 Missing Stochastic Energy Modeling
Energy inputs are not modeled as stochastic processes:
- renewable variability
- fuel cost fluctuations
- demand uncertainty

### 6.4 Undefined Protocol Specification Language
“Multiprotocol scheduling” lacks:
- formal schema definition
- constraint language
- arbitration semantics

### 6.5 Incomplete Economic Model
Missing:
- explicit pricing functions
- arbitrage constraints
- revenue stability modeling

### 6.6 Network and Data Locality Abstraction Missing
No formal treatment of:
- distributed inference latency
- model sharding costs
- data movement overhead

### 6.7 Safety and Fault Tolerance Not Fully Specified
Missing:
- thermal runaway handling
- battery failure modes
- generator fault isolation
- degraded mode behavior

---

## 7. Design Implications

### 7.1 Compute Becomes a Physical Control Variable
Compute is no longer abstract—it directly modulates:
- generator efficiency
- thermal stability
- energy buffering systems

### 7.2 Scheduler Becomes Core System
The scheduler evolves into:
- control plane
- economic engine
- thermal regulator
- energy arbitrage system

### 7.3 Infrastructure Becomes Programmable Energy System
The stack behaves as:
> a software-defined energy conversion system using computation as its output channel

---

## 9. Conclusion

The DARE architecture proposes a unified system in which computation is embedded within energy generation, storage, and thermal systems as a controllable and economically optimized transformation process.

Across its components, the system converges toward:

> a distributed, energy-native operating system for dispatchable computation

The central innovation is not any single subsystem, but the integration of:

- energy systems  
- thermal systems  
- compute systems  
- economic systems  
- scheduling systems  

into a single feedback-controlled optimization loop.

This reframes computing infrastructure as a **thermodynamic economic engine**, rather than a static computational resource pool.
